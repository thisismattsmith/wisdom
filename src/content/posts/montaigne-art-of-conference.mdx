<Hero eyebrow="An Essay" title={"The Art\nof Conference"}>
  Montaigne loved argument. Not the kind that ends with someone retreating to lick wounds, but the kind that makes both sides smarter than they were when they started. He spent an entire essay on what separates the two.
</Hero>

<SourceQuote author="Michel de Montaigne" work="Essays, Book III, Chapter VIII — Of the Art of Conference" year="1588">
  "When any one contradicts me, he raises my attention, not my anger: I advance towards him who controverts, who instructs me; the cause of truth ought to be the common cause both of the one and the other… We evade correction, whereas we ought to offer and present ourselves to it, especially when it appears in the form of conference, and not of authority. At every opposition, we do not consider whether or no it be just, but, right or wrong, how to disengage ourselves: instead of extending the arms, we thrust out our claws… Nevertheless, it is hard to bring the men of my time to it: they have not the courage to correct, because they have not the courage to suffer themselves to be corrected."
</SourceQuote>
