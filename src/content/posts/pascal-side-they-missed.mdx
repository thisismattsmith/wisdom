<Hero eyebrow="Two Fragments" title={"Show Them the Side\nThey Missed"}>
  Pascal's <em>Pensées</em> were never published in his lifetime — they're fragments left on his desk when he died in 1662. Two of those fragments, numbered 9 and 10 in the standard Trotter translation, together form the sharpest practical advice on persuasion in this library.
</Hero>

<SourceQuote author="Blaise Pascal" work="Pensées, fragments 9 & 10" year="1670" label="The Fragments">
  <strong>9.</strong> "When we wish to correct with advantage, and to show another that he errs, we must notice from what side he views the matter, for on that side it is usually true, and admit that truth to him, but reveal to him the side on which it is false. He is satisfied with that, for he sees that he was not mistaken, and that he only failed to see all sides. Now, no one is offended at not seeing everything; but one does not like to be mistaken."
  <br /><br />
  <strong>10.</strong> "People are generally better persuaded by the reasons which they have themselves discovered than by those which have come into the mind of others."
</SourceQuote>
